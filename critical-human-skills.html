1Adaptability

Thriving Through Rapid Technological Change

Action steps

1

Build a structured continuous learning habit

Dedicate 30 minutes per day to learning something new about AI, your industry, or adjacent skills. The discipline of showing up consistently matters more than the content.

Starting points:

  • Schedule a recurring "learning block" in your calendar — protect it like a meeting
  • Rotate formats: one week read, one week watch, one week do
  • Keep a learning log: one sentence per day on what you absorbed
Measurable outcome: Complete 1 structured course per quarter. Log 60 days of learning entries.
2

Run deliberate adoption experiments

When a new tool, process, or methodology appears, resist the reflexive "not for me." Commit to a 2-week trial with a defined success condition.

Experiment template:

  • Name the thing you've been avoiding
  • Define what "gave it a fair shot" looks like
  • Set a 2-week timeframe with specific tasks to complete
  • Write a 3-sentence retrospective: what worked, what didn't, will I continue?
Measurable outcome: Complete 3 deliberate adoption experiments per year.
3

Build a personal learning network

The fastest learners don't learn alone. Surround yourself with people who pull you forward.

Ways to build yours:

  • Join 2–3 online communities active in AI and your field (LinkedIn groups, Discord, Slack)
  • Attend one industry event or webinar per month
  • Share one insight with your team each week — teaching accelerates your own learning
Measurable outcome: Join 2 communities, attend 4 events or webinars, and share one insight per week for one month.

2Communication

Communicating Across Disciplines and Roles

Action steps

1

Practice AI output translation

AI rarely produces something ready for its intended audience. The skill is knowing how to translate it. Take any AI-generated output and rewrite it for three distinct audiences.

Translation exercise:

  • For your team — full context, technical where appropriate
  • For a non-technical stakeholder — no jargon, outcome-first framing
  • For leadership — 3 sentences max: what, so what, now what

Apply the "so what" test to every AI-generated insight before sharing it.

Measurable outcome: Complete 5 three-audience translation exercises.
2

Develop your AI storytelling vocabulary

Learn to talk about AI honestly — its possibilities and its limits — without either hype or fear. People trust communicators who acknowledge uncertainty.

Phrases to practice:

  • "AI suggested this, and here's why I agree / disagree…"
  • "This is a draft from AI that I've reviewed and edited."
  • "I used AI to speed up the research; here's my interpretation."
  • "This is a 70% confidence output — I'd want to validate the key claims."
Measurable outcome: Use at least 3 of these framing phrases in real workplace conversations over the next 2 weeks.
3

Strengthen cross-functional collaboration

AI tools increasingly span department boundaries. The ability to work across disciplines is no longer optional.

Build this muscle:

  • Volunteer for a cross-functional AI project outside your primary domain
  • Schedule monthly coffee chats with someone in a different department
  • Create a one-page "how I work with AI" document to share with collaborators
Measurable outcome: Lead or meaningfully participate in 2 cross-functional AI-related projects.

3Creativity

Using AI as a Creative Collaborator

Action steps

1

Use AI for divergence, humans for convergence

AI is a powerful idea generator. Humans are better at knowing which ideas are actually worth pursuing. Use AI to generate quantity, then apply your judgment.

The 100-idea exercise:

  1. Pick a real challenge you're working on
  2. Ask AI to generate 100 variations, angles, or ideas
  3. Read through without editing — let it wash over you
  4. Highlight the 5 that surprise you or feel generative
  5. Combine and remix those 5 in ways AI wouldn't
Measurable outcome: Run the 100-idea exercise on 3 different real projects.
2

Never use the first output as final

The first AI draft is a starting point, not an endpoint. The creative gap between "AI output" and "great work" is where your value lives.

Iteration prompts to build the habit:

  • "What are the weaknesses in this approach?"
  • "Give me a completely different angle on this."
  • "What would someone who disagrees with this say?"
  • "Make this more surprising / more opinionated / more concrete."

Human vs. AI in the creative process

Human leads

Taste and curation — knowing what's worth pursuing. Emotional resonance. Breaking conventions with intent. Unique lived perspective.

AI assists

Generating high volume rapidly. Exploring style variations. First-draft scaffolding. Surfacing patterns from large datasets.

Measurable outcome: Complete 10 AI-assisted creative tasks where you iterate beyond the first draft before finalizing.
3

Cultivate a creative signature

AI can mimic many styles, but it can't replicate you. Your creative signature — the perspective, aesthetic, and values that make your work distinctly yours — becomes more valuable as AI output becomes ubiquitous.

Build yours:

  • Collect 20 examples of work (yours or others') that resonates — what do they share?
  • Write 3 sentences that describe your creative values or aesthetic principles
  • Use AI to execute your vision, not define it
Measurable outcome: Write your creative values statement and apply it to guide one AI-assisted project from brief to final output.

4Problem-Solving

Complex Decision-Making in Ambiguous Situations

Action steps

1

Invest in problem definition before solution generation

AI is excellent at generating solutions — but only for the problem you describe. A poorly framed problem produces a plausible-sounding wrong answer. Spend 80% of your time on the question before handing it to AI.

Framing toolkit:

  • Use "5 Whys" to find root causes before jumping to solutions
  • Define what success looks like before generating options
  • List constraints and non-negotiables explicitly
  • Ask: "Am I solving the right problem, or the presenting problem?"
Measurable outcome: Apply structured problem-framing to 5 real challenges, documenting the shift from presenting to root problem.
2

Develop emotional intelligence in AI-augmented teams

AI adoption creates anxiety, status uncertainty, and skill displacement fears. The ability to read the room and manage the human dynamics of change is irreplaceable.

Practice areas:

  • Acknowledge change anxiety openly — name it before problem-solving it
  • Practice active listening when team members express concerns about AI tools
  • Use AI to reduce administrative friction, freeing time for human conversation
  • Complete an EQ self-assessment to identify personal growth areas
Measurable outcome: Complete one EQ assessment and identify 2 specific behaviors to practice over 90 days.
3

Build a personal decision framework

Develop a consistent process for making high-stakes decisions when information is incomplete — a condition AI will not solve for you.

Framework elements to develop:

  • Document the criteria you use when you make good decisions
  • Identify your top 3 cognitive biases using a structured assessment
  • Practice "red-teaming": argue the strongest case against your own intuition
  • Use AI to surface perspectives and data points you haven't considered — then decide yourself
Measurable outcome: Document your decision criteria and complete one red-team exercise on a real high-stakes decision.
