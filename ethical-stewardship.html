1Verification

Fact-Checking and Validating AI Outputs

Action steps

1

Apply a verification checklist before acting on any AI output

The key question to ask yourself: "Would I stake my reputation on this being accurate?" If not, it needs verification before you act on it or share it.

Personal verification checklist:

  • Is this a specific claim — a number, date, quote, or named citation?
  • Is it time-sensitive or related to recent events?
  • Does it affect a decision with real consequences?
  • Have I checked at least one independent source?
  • Could the AI have confidently guessed here?
Measurable outcome: Apply your verification checklist to 20 AI outputs over 30 days and log where verification changed or confirmed the output.
2

Learn to recognize AI hallucination patterns

AI generates confident-sounding falsehoods — called hallucinations — most often in predictable contexts. Knowing the patterns makes them easier to catch.

Where hallucinations are most common:

  • Specific statistics, percentages, and numerical data
  • Named quotes attributed to real people
  • Citations and references — titles, authors, DOIs
  • Very recent events (post training-data cutoff)
  • Niche, obscure, or highly technical topics

Practice exercise: Ask AI for 5 specific facts or sources on a topic you know well, then verify each independently and log what it got wrong.

Measurable outcome: Complete 5 hallucination-spotting exercises, documenting what the AI got wrong and the category of error.
3

Build source-evaluation skills using the SIFT method

When verifying AI outputs, the quality of your sources matters. Not all sources used to check a claim are equal. Use the SIFT method as a consistent evaluation framework.

SIFT:

  • Stop — pause before sharing or acting; notice your emotional reaction
  • Investigate the source — who is behind it? What's their credibility and purpose?
  • Find better coverage — look for multiple independent sources making the same claim
  • Trace claims — follow information back to the original, primary source
Measurable outcome: Apply the SIFT method to 15 sources across 3 weeks and document your source quality ratings.

2Bias Awareness

Recognizing and Mitigating Algorithmic Bias

Action steps

1

Understand how bias enters AI systems

Bias in AI is not a bug — it's a reflection of the data, decisions, and contexts that shaped the system. Understanding its origins is the first step to recognizing it in practice.

Common bias types to know:

  • Representation bias: certain groups are underrepresented in training data
  • Measurement bias: the data collected uses flawed proxies for what it claims to measure
  • Historical bias: AI learns from data that reflects past injustices or inequities
  • Aggregation bias: one-size-fits-all models applied to populations with real differences
  • Deployment bias: a model used in a context it wasn't designed or tested for
Measurable outcome: Identify one real-world example of each bias type from current AI tools or published AI audits.
2

Run structured bias tests on tools you use

Bias in AI tools is often invisible until you actively probe for it. Build the habit of testing tools before trusting them for consequential tasks.

Test techniques:

  • Vary names, apparent genders, and locations in otherwise identical prompts — does the output change?
  • Test with different languages or cultural contexts for the same underlying request
  • Check whether the tool handles edge cases that affect underrepresented groups
  • Compare responses when describing the same person with different demographic markers
Measurable outcome: Complete 3 systematic bias audits on tools you use regularly. Document findings and share with your team.
3

Build a fairness lens into your AI workflow

Before deploying or sharing any significant AI-generated output, run it through a fairness review. This is especially important when AI influences decisions about people.

Fairness review questions:

  • Who benefits from this output? Who might be disadvantaged?
  • Whose perspective or experience is absent from this output?
  • If this were wrong in a particular direction, who would be most harmed?
  • Would I be comfortable if the people affected could see how this was produced?
Measurable outcome: Apply a fairness review to 10 AI-generated outputs and document at least 3 instances where the review changed or improved the final output.

3Privacy

Data Privacy and Responsible AI Use

Action steps

1

Understand what AI tools collect and retain

Most commercial AI tools retain input data — often for model training — unless you explicitly configure otherwise or use an enterprise tier. "Private" and "deleted" do not always mean what you expect.

Four data categories to understand for every tool you use:

  • Input data: what you type, upload, or speak
  • Output data: what the tool generates in response
  • Training data: whether your inputs can be used to retrain the model
  • Metadata: timestamps, usage patterns, device and location data
Measurable outcome: Review the privacy policy and data retention settings for 5 AI tools in your regular workflow. Document what each retains and whether opt-outs exist.
2

Apply data minimization principles

Share only what the task strictly requires. Before uploading a document or pasting data into an AI tool, ask: does the AI actually need this to complete the task?

Never share with external AI tools (without explicit approval):

  • Personally identifiable information (PII) about colleagues, customers, or students
  • Health records, financial data, or legal documents
  • Confidential business strategy, pricing, or unreleased product information
  • Client data covered by contractual or regulatory obligations

Before sharing data with AI

Safe practice

Anonymize documents first. Replace names with "Person A." Remove account numbers, dates of birth, and contact details. Then run the AI task on the scrubbed version.

Avoid

Uploading raw HR records, client contracts, or research data to consumer AI tools. Even if you delete the conversation, the data may already have been logged.

Measurable outcome: Conduct a data minimization audit of your current AI tool usage — identify and stop 3 habits where you're sharing more data than necessary.
3

Know and apply your organization's AI data policies

Your personal judgment about privacy is a starting point — not the final word. Organizations have legal and contractual obligations that go further.

Build your policy awareness:

  • Locate and read your organization's AI acceptable-use policy
  • Identify which AI tools are approved for which data sensitivity levels
  • Know who to contact when you're unsure — don't guess when data is at stake
  • Create a quick-reference card: "For [data type], I use [approved tool] and avoid [unapproved tools]"
Measurable outcome: Map 5 AI tools you currently use against your organization's data classification policy and address any gaps you find.

4Critical Thinking

Questioning AI-Driven Recommendations

Action steps

1

Build a challenge-first reflex

Treat every AI recommendation as a hypothesis, not a conclusion. The habit of asking one challenge question before accepting any significant AI output is one of the most protective behaviors you can build.

Challenge questions to have ready:

  • "What's the basis for this claim?"
  • "What's the strongest counter-argument?"
  • "What is this output not telling me?"
  • "If I'm wrong to trust this, what's the cost?"
  • "Would I reach this conclusion without AI?"
Measurable outcome: Document 15 instances where a challenge question improved, corrected, or meaningfully altered the final output.
2

Know when and how to escalate AI concerns

Not every AI problem is one you can or should solve alone. Knowing when to escalate — and to whom — is a professional skill that protects you and your organization.

Escalation triggers:

  • Factual errors in high-stakes or customer-facing outputs
  • AI-driven decisions that appear to discriminate or disadvantage a group
  • Privacy concerns — data shared that shouldn't have been
  • AI outputs that could cause harm if acted upon
  • Pressure to use AI in ways that conflict with policy or your ethical judgment
Measurable outcome: Write a one-page personal AI concern escalation protocol — what you raise, to whom, and under what conditions. Review it with your manager.
3

Develop your independent reasoning practice

The most calibrated AI users form their own view first, then consult AI. Over time this builds a reliable sense of when AI output is trustworthy and when it needs scrutiny.

The "independent first" exercise:

  1. Read the question or problem
  2. Write your own answer or hypothesis first — do not consult AI yet
  3. Then ask AI the same question
  4. Compare: where do you agree? Where do you diverge?
  5. Investigate divergences — who was right, and why?

Keep a calibration log: over time you'll learn the domains where AI is reliable for you and where your own judgment is sharper.

Measurable outcome: Complete 10 "independent first" reasoning exercises and identify 2 domains where AI consistently outperforms your first instinct.
